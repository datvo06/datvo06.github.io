The co-design problem

Given a task: design a morphology, and control that morphology to finish the task.

Karl Sims, Evolving Virtual Creatures (1994): the body and its brain evolved together.

A closer look: Evolving Virtual Creatures (Sims 1994)

The genotype: a nested directed graph
Graph // the body
  ├─ Node = body part { shape, joint }
  │   └─ brain : Graph // of neurons
  └─ Edge = connection { position, scale, reflect }
Body and brain are the same kind of object: a directed graph nested inside a directed graph.
The control: a dataflow from sensors to effectors
Control : Sensors → Effectors

Sensor   = joint-angle | contact(±1) | photo
Neuron   = f(inputs),  f ∈ { sum, product, min, max,
                    if, sin, cos, atan, log, ... }
        + recurrent state
Effector = weight · neuron → joint force

Genotype graphs and the morphologies they develop into (Sims 1994, Fig. 1). Self-loops and connection counts set how parts repeat.

The objective is a hand-written fitness

No reward model, no gradient: one scalar per behavior, computed after a ~10s simulation

Swimming: distance of the center of mass per unit time; max distance from start (rewards straight over circling); final-phase velocity weighted higher (rewards sustained motion).
Swimming: the one quantity the paper names
$$ F_{\text{swim}} \;\propto\; \max_{0 \le t \le T} \lVert c(t) - c(0) \rVert $$

What the search produced

Many separate evolutions, each 50 to 100 generations; population 300, survival ratio 1/5

Swimming: paddlers, flippers, sinusoidal watersnakes.

Walking: lizard gaits, inchworms, hoppers.

Following: steering fins that turn toward the light (green target).

Needs 100-generation runs: about 3 hours on a 32-processor CM-5.

What have changed since then?

More computation
A single laptop GPU now delivers tens of TFLOPS: on the order of 10,000× the peak of the 32-node CM-5.
More powerful techniques
Neural networks, differentiable simulation, foundation models, and LLMs now do work the genetic algorithm could not.
More ambition
And harder goals: long-horizon tasks on real hardware, not just locomotion of a block creature.

Dexterous manipulation

Realistic, manufacturable robots

2.1 Bi-level optimization

Neural networks let us train much more powerful controllers, per morphology

Outer loop · morphology
propose a body  ↓
Inner loop · control
train a controller for this body, from scratch
deep RL, e.g. Heess et al. 2017
score the pair  ↓  step the body, repeat

cost = bodies explored × one full controller training each. DERL trains a fresh 5-million-step RL controller for each of about 4000 evolved bodies (Gupta et al. 2021): roughly 20 billion environment steps, 1152 CPUs, 16 hours. A body's controller does not transfer to the next.

DERL · different evolved bodies (DoF 14 to 20), each with an RL controller trained for it (Gupta et al. 2021)

Captures: the true coupling of body and controller; principled scoring of a design.
Misses: cost. Every candidate pays a full inner-loop training run, and none of it transfers to the next body.

2.2 Learned graph surrogates

Learning away the cost, 1 of 3: learn the evaluator. A grammar over robot graphs plus a GNN that predicts performance

RoboGrammar and friends make the design space compact and search-friendly: a grammar generates robot graphs, and a learned model scores them without running a full simulation each time.

Zhao et al., RoboGrammar (SIGGRAPH Asia 2020)

Captures: a compact, searchable design space; fast approximate evaluation.
Misses: grounding. The graph has no real geometry, joint limits, or ports, and the surrogate needs training data we do not have in new domains.

2.3 Differentiable simulation

Learning away the cost, 2 of 3: learn the controller (a neural network), by backpropagating through the physics

The loop, for one body
Write the physics engine so it is differentiable. Then: run the body forward for a few seconds, measure a task loss (how far it walked, how much it turned the knob), and backpropagate that loss through every simulated step and contact back to the controller's weights. Gradient descent nudges the weights. Repeat.
What the gradient trains
A neural-network policy, one per body. Not MPC (that plans online, no learning). Not model-free RL (that estimates gradients from reward samples). Here the gradient is exact, through the physics, so it needs far fewer rollouts.

ELDiR (2024): a fresh policy trained per body, through differentiable simulation

Captures: exact gradients train a strong controller for a body far more cheaply than sampling.
Misses: it needs a purpose-built differentiable physics engine, and only works on bodies whose dynamics differentiate cleanly. Stiff rigid contact and friction, the grasping and in-hand manipulation we care about, are non-smooth, so the gradient turns biased or exploding right where it is needed.
collision: a jump
add a part: discrete step

2.3b Foundation controllers: don't train one per body

Still learning away the controller, but once, so a new body reuses it instead of training from scratch

The problem it solves
Differentiable sim and RL both train a policy from scratch for every candidate body. That is the bi-level cost: thousands of bodies times one full training run each.
The idea: pretrain once
Train one morphology-agnostic controller across many bodies. It takes the body's description as input, so it already knows how to drive a body it has never seen. A new candidate reuses the pretrained policy, no per-body training.
What it buys
Evaluating a design becomes nearly free, so the outer search can explore non-differentiable body edits (add, remove, recombine parts) and score each one immediately.

Morphological pretraining (Strgar & Kriegman 2025): one controller drives many different bodies

The same idea at scale: foundation controllers. Universal policies, diffusion policies, and vision-language-action models are pretrained on many robots and tasks, then reused or lightly adapted on a new one.
Adds: amortizes the controller across bodies, so evaluating a candidate design is nearly free.
Misses: the controller is not designed per morphology, one shared policy drives all of them. So how well it drives a given body depends on how much of the morphology space the pretraining has seen; a body far from that distribution is driven poorly.

2.4 LLM-driven morphology design

LLMs have a very good prior

Lang2Morph (Qiao et al. 2025): an LLM maps a natural-language task through a structural hand grammar to a 3D-printable, task-specific hand.

Figure: Qiao et al., Lang2Morph (arXiv:2509.18937)

Nearby LLM-era efforts: VLMgineer (robot tools), A Design Co-Pilot (task-tailored manipulators), Text2Touch (LLM-designed rewards), House of Dextra (cross-embodied dexterous-hand co-design).

Captures: cheap iteration; natural-language task input; manufacturable output.
Misses: co-design itself. The controller is frozen, and the task often needs morphology and control to move together.

Each family learns away one role; the roles stay coupled. Co-design at LLM speed needs a substrate that holds all three.

What we still need: three desiderata

1
A substrate that can describe both abstract and realistic, manufacturable robots.
2
A way to put the LLM's prior to work navigating that design space.
3
A tool to describe transferable control, and to do it efficiently, no from-scratch training per body.

How we approach co-design

Two components, the next two parts of this talk

01 · The representation

A programming language expressive enough to describe abstract components and realistic, manufacturable robots, uncertainty over the designs, and the control objective, all in one program.

One program can be rendered to CAD, compiled to MuJoCo, constraint-solved in JAX, and controlled by MPC. A palm can declare a prior over where its fingers attach; declare a loss and the controller comes for free.

02 · The LLM framework

An LLM framework that takes full advantage of that representation: generation as an effect, so proposing a design is writing a program.

An LLM call is a typed function call: returns are parsed and validated, tools and sub-templates are captured from lexical scope, and a template can even return a function. Handlers add retries, providers, and checks without touching the program.

@defcomponent
def Arm(self, link1L=0.120, link2L=0.1, linkDia=0.025):
    """Two-link arm with revolute joints."""
    self.link1       = Cylinder(d=linkDia, h=link1L)
    self.link2       = Cylinder(d=linkDia, h=link2L)
    self.shoulder    = HingeJoint(plateD=0.032)
    self.elbow       = HingeJoint(plateD=0.032)
    self.wristFlange = Cylinder(d=0.032, h=0.005)

    # Ports + welds compose the kinematic chain:
    self.base = self.place(Port())
    self._weld_shoulder = joints.Weld(self.base,         self.shoulder.base)
    self._weld_link1    = joints.Weld(self.shoulder.tip, self.link1.base)

    # rotate elbow frame so its hinge axis points along Y
    pose = Pose(rv=jnp.pi / 2 * jnp.array([0, 0, 1]))
    self._weld_elbow    = joints.Weld(self.link1.tip,  self.elbow.base, pose=pose)
    self._weld_link2    = joints.Weld(self.elbow.tip,  self.link2.base, pose=~pose)
    self._weld_wrist    = joints.Weld(self.link2.tip,  self.wristFlange.base)

    self.tip = self.wristFlange.tip   # downstream tasks reach with this port

def CircularPalm(self, n_fingers=3, ...):
    """Fingers Dirichlet-sampled on the front
    semicircle [-pi/2, +pi/2]."""
    spread = math.pi

    gaps = choose(dist.Dirichlet(jnp.ones(n_fingers + 1)))

    finger_angles = -spread/2 + spread * jnp.cumsum(gaps[:-1])
    _build_disc_palm(self, finger_angles=finger_angles, ...)

What unlocks cheap codesign: MPC + a loss interface

Model predictive control: plan a short horizon with the model, execute, replan

MPC in one line
At every control step, use the model to plan a short trajectory into the future, execute the first piece, and replan. No training: the controller is search, run online.

One run of control_cube_spinning.ipynb, re-executed for this slide (papermill): 8 seconds at dt = 0.01, batch-64 MPPI. The declared total and per-term losses (nodrop, nolinvel, offaxis, onaxis) settle to zero as the spin stabilizes.

@pydantic.dataclasses.dataclass
class Rating:
    score: int
    explanation: str

    @field_validator("score")
    @classmethod
    def check_score(cls, v):
        if v < 1 or v > 5:
            raise PydanticCustomError(
                "invalid_score", "score must be 1-5, got {v}", {"v": v})
        return v

    @field_validator("explanation")
    @classmethod
    def check_explanation_contains_score(cls, v, info):
        score = info.data.get("score", None)
        if score is not None and str(score) not in v:
            raise PydanticCustomError("invalid_explanation",
                "explanation must mention the score {score}",
                {"score": score})
        return v

@dataclasses.dataclass
class ChatBot(Agent):
    """Friendly bot named {self.bot_name}."""

    bot_name: str = "ChatBot"

    @Tool.define                            # a plain operation
    def remember(self, fact: str) -> str:
        """Persist a fact about the user."""
        print(f"[tool] remember({fact!r})")
        return f"noted: {fact}"

    @Template.define                        # an LLM-implemented op
    def send(self, user_input: str) -> str:
        """User writes: {user_input}.
        Use your remember tool to keep facts across turns."""
        raise NotHandled


bot = ChatBot()
with handler(LiteLLMProvider()):            # bind the meaning
    print(bot.send("Hi! I am in France."))
    print(bot.send("Where am I, again?"))   # sees prior turn

The LLM in the design loop

  
Because designs are programs, proposing a design is writing a program. The LLM writes and revises RoboTL; the harness around it is the same handler mechanism that interprets the designs.

  

    
Errors are loud. Port mismatches and weld misalignments fail at construction, not after a day of simulation.

    
Variation is parametric. A new candidate is a changed default, not a redrawn model.

    
The simulator answers in seconds. Fast feedback closes the propose-and-revise loop.

def aux_losses(env) -> dict:
    tip = env.robot.tip.pos
    target = env.target.pos
    entrance = jnp.array([0.0, 1.1, target[2] - 0.01])
    pregrasp = jnp.array([0.0, target[1] - 0.03, target[2]])
    t = sim_time()
    s1 = jnp.clip(t / 1.0, 0.0, 1.0)
    s2 = jnp.clip((t - 1.0) / 1.5, 0.0, 1.0)
    guide = (1.0 - s1) * entrance + s1 * ((1.0 - s2) * pregrasp + s2 * target)
    return {
        "staged_centerline_insert": Equalize(tip, guide, weight=0.012),
        "forward_progress": Maximize(tip[1], weight=0.008),
        "depth_match": Nullify(tip[1] - target[1], weight=0.02),
        "height_match": Nullify(tip[2] - target[2], weight=0.015),
        "soft_center_x": Nullify(tip[0], weight=0.006),
        "entrance_band_x": KeepBelow(jnp.abs(tip[0]), 0.05, weight=0.015),
    }

  Ablation: keep the same morphology, but remove the designed objective. The system fails. Both pieces matter together.

Future work: learning to describe the environment

  
Predicator v3, a case study in progress: an agent writes the environment description against demonstration data.

  

    

      
simulator.py

      Low-level continuous dynamics: per-step process rules with learnable constants, fitted by MCMC. Rigid-body physics stays trusted; only what physics does not explain is learned.
    

    

      
predicates.py

      High-level symbolic concepts: classifiers that mark when a subgoal becomes true, feeding bilevel planning and refinement.
    

  

  

    
The two share fitted parameters: the process's firing gate and the planner's subgoal check are the same learned threshold. Concepts and dynamics agree by construction.
